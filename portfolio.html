The production portfolio

What to build, how to save it, how to organize it, and how to hand it in.
Inés Thiebaut · CSU East Bay

Your portfolio is the collected body of finished work you make while you study, kept in one organized place and added to every semester. It is how your progress is reviewed, and it is the first professional record of what you can do: the thing you show to artists you want to work with, to labels, and to schools after you leave.

What your portfolio contains

Two things travel with every song: the finished master, and a short note about it. Alongside the songs, the portfolio always holds a current short biography.

Every song

Each song in the portfolio travels as three files:

  • The finished master. Your final stereo mixdown, exported as lossless audio (formats below). This is the version anyone would hear if they played the song.
  • The DAW project file. The session in your DAW's own format. This is the working master you go back to when you revise, re-export, or pull a stem.
  • A production note, as a PDF. A short document that stands in for the score a producer's work does not have.

Keep the production note brief, and cover:

  • Tempo, key, and duration.
  • The instruments and plugins you used.
  • Where your sounds came from, and whether any sample is cleared or royalty-free.
  • Who did what, with each person's ownership percentage if you made the song with others. This is your split sheet: writing it down while the song is fresh saves the argument later.

Always in the portfolio

A short biography in two parts: a paragraph introducing you as a producer, and one or two sentences on what your music is and who it is for. Save it as a PDF and update it as your work grows.

File types and formats

The portfolio is an archive, so its files need to last and to open cleanly on any machine. Two formats do most of the work: lossless audio for the masters, and PDF for anything on the page.

Masters as lossless audio

Lossless means the file keeps the full recording with nothing thrown away. It comes in two kinds, both fine for the portfolio: uncompressed (WAV, AIFF), which stores the audio at full size, and compressed (FLAC, ALAC), which shrinks the file and rebuilds the exact original signal on playback, so you save space and lose nothing.

Save each finished master as:

  • Format: WAV or AIFF, or FLAC or ALAC. Any of the four is fine.
  • Sample rate and bit depth: 48 kHz, 24-bit.
  • Channels: stereo.

Keep lossy formats, MP3 and AAC, out of the portfolio. They make the file small by permanently discarding sound, and that loss cannot be undone. An MP3 is fine for casual sharing, but let the lossless file be the copy that lives in the portfolio.

A word on loudness

Streaming platforms turn every song up or down to a shared loudness when they play it, so squashing your master as loud as it will go wins nothing and costs you dynamics. Master so the song sounds right, not so the meter reads highest. Keep the archived master at its natural level and let the platform do the leveling.

Project files

Keep each project file in your DAW's own format, like Ableton's .als, next to its master. The lossless file is what people hear; the project file is what you edit. Keeping both means you can come back months later to change a mix, bounce a new version, or pull a stem.

Your biography as PDF

Save your biography as a PDF, so the formatting stays put wherever it opens. Keep the editable original, the Word or Pages file, so you can update it each semester and export a fresh PDF when you do.

Easy to get wrong

Two things trip people up:

  • Save your masters as lossless audio (WAV or FLAC), rather than MP3.
  • Keep the project file next to each master, not just the exported audio.

Organizing your files

A portfolio is only as useful as it is easy to open and read in order. Keep one folder for the whole portfolio, one subfolder per song, numbered so they sort the way you want them seen. Name every file so its owner, its song, and what it is are clear from the name alone.

The naming rule is the one from your first day: lowercase, hyphens instead of spaces, and none of the characters that break on other systems, like slashes, colons, quotes, and apostrophes. A workable structure looks like this:

lastname-portfolio/
  01-song-title/
    lastname-song-title-master.wav
    lastname-song-title-project.als
    lastname-song-title-notes.pdf
  02-song-title/
    lastname-song-title-master.wav
    lastname-song-title-project.als
    lastname-song-title-notes.pdf
  bio/
    lastname-bio.pdf

A few habits keep the names clean and sortable:

  • Number each song folder with two digits (01, 02, 10) so they hold their order. A single digit puts 10 ahead of 2.
  • Put the same information in every file name, in the same order: your last name, the song, and what the file is.
  • Keep spelling and capitalization steady, so a title reads the same way everywhere it appears.

Submitting your portfolio

Submit the whole portfolio as a single compressed file, a zip. Zipping bundles the entire folder into one file you upload in one step, and it keeps your folder structure and file names intact for whoever opens it.

Make the zip

  • Mac: right-click the top portfolio folder and choose Compress.
  • Windows: right-click the folder, choose Send to, then Compressed (zipped) folder.

Either one makes a single .zip next to your folder. Name it with your last name, the word portfolio, and the semester, for example lastname-portfolio-fall2026.zip.

Check it before you hand it in

Open your own zip before you submit it. Unzip it somewhere new and confirm:

  • Every song folder is there.
  • Every master plays.
  • Every note and your bio open.

A zip missing a file, or holding a master that will not play, is the most common problem at submission, and testing your own copy first catches it. Keep your organized portfolio folder on your own computer, and back it up somewhere else as well.

Keeping it current

Update the portfolio as you go, not the night before it is due. When you finish a song, add its master, its project file, and its note right away, while the files are in front of you. Keep your project files in their native format so you can revise and re-export later.

Treat the portfolio as a public document, because it becomes one. The same organized, clearly labeled, well-made set of songs that carries you through your studies is what you send to artists, labels, and schools when you leave.
