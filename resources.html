Resources

Getting your music heard, getting paid for it, and getting better at it.
Inés Thiebaut · CSU East Bay

A working list of the tools and organizations that carry a track from your machine out into the world: how it reaches streaming platforms, how the money it earns finds its way back to you, and where to keep learning once you have the basics down. Much of this is the business side of producing, the part that rarely comes up while you are making the beat.

Before you dive in

You do not need any of this to make music, and you do not need all of it at once. Read a section when it becomes relevant: distribution when you have a track ready to release, rights and royalties the first time something of yours goes onto a streaming platform. Most of these services are free or cheap for someone starting out, and most are based in the United States.

Getting your music heard

Streaming platforms do not let you upload directly. You go through a distributor, a service that delivers your finished track, artwork, and track information to Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon, and the rest, and then collects the sound recording royalty those plays earn and pays it to you. You keep ownership of your music. The distributor takes either a flat yearly fee or a small cut, never your rights.

The three below are the ones most independent artists use. They differ mainly in how they charge: a yearly subscription for unlimited releases, or a one-time fee per release. Pick based on how often you plan to put music out.

  • DistroKid: a yearly subscription for unlimited releases, fast to the stores. Best if you release often.
  • TuneCore: yearly plans with fuller reporting and a publishing option attached.
  • CD Baby: a one-time fee per release that keeps the music up for good, no subscription. Best if you release rarely.

Distributors deliver your track to the big platforms. A few others work differently: you upload straight to them, and they double as the place listeners discover you and the place your most committed fans support you directly. For many producers this is where a track first meets an audience, well before it reaches Spotify.

  • SoundCloud: where the producer, beat, and electronic scenes live. Post demos, remixes, and edits directly, get comments, and build a following. Its paid tier also distributes to the streaming platforms and pays fan-powered royalties on plays.
  • Bandcamp: a direct storefront where fans buy your music and you keep most of the sale. Strong for EPs, beat and sample packs, vinyl, and superfan support.

Rights and royalties

When you make a track you create two different things, and the law treats them separately. One is the song itself, the composition: its melody, chords, and any lyrics. The other is the specific recording of that song, the master. Each earns money in its own way, and each is collected by a different organization. Understanding the split is what keeps you from leaving money on the table or signing away more than you meant to.

Every time your track streams it can generate up to three separate payments, and they do not all arrive from the same place. A performing rights organization (PRO) collects the performance royalty on the composition. The Mechanical Licensing Collective collects the mechanical royalty on the composition. Your distributor collects the sound recording royalty on the master and passes it to you. Sign up with only one and you collect only part of what the track earns.

A PRO pays you when your music is performed in public, streamed, or broadcast. You affiliate with one, register your songs, and it tracks plays and sends what you are owed. Affiliate with a single PRO, since you belong to one at a time.

  • ASCAP: a PRO you can join directly, free for writers.
  • BMI: a PRO you can join directly, free for writers.
  • SESAC: a PRO you join by invitation.

The mechanical royalty is the other half of the composition's streaming income, and in the United States a nonprofit called the Mechanical Licensing Collective collects it. Membership is free, you keep everything it collects for you, and a PRO does not cover this: they are separate payments, so you register with both.

  • The MLC: collects U.S. streaming mechanical royalties on the composition. Free to join through its Connect to Collect signup.
  • SoundExchange: collects royalties on the master from digital and satellite radio, a stream your distributor does not cover.

Registering with a PRO, the MLC, and collection societies abroad is a lot of separate accounts to keep straight. A publishing administrator does that work for you: it registers your songs everywhere and collects on your behalf, usually for a small percentage. It is worth knowing about once you have a few releases out.

  • Songtrust: a publishing administrator that registers and collects your composition royalties worldwide for a cut.
Split sheets

When you make a track with other people, a split sheet is the one page that records who contributed and what percentage each person owns, signed before the track goes anywhere. It sounds like paperwork you can put off. It is the single best way to avoid the most common and most bitter arguments in music, and it is far easier to agree on splits in the room where the track was made than a year later when it starts earning.

If you sample or sell beats

Sampling someone else's recording without permission is using their master, and releasing it that way can get your track pulled or land you a bill. Clearing a sample means getting permission from whoever owns the recording and the composition, and it is often expensive or slow. For anything you plan to release, the safe paths are royalty-free sample libraries, sounds you record and manipulate yourself, or a sample you have actually cleared.

If you make beats to sell, a marketplace lets you lease or sell them outright. A lease is non-exclusive: you license the same beat to many artists, each paying a smaller amount. An exclusive sale hands the beat to one buyer and takes it off the market, for much more. Know which one you are selling before you agree on a price.

  • BeatStars: the largest marketplace for leasing and selling beats, with contract templates built in.
  • Airbit: a second established beat marketplace, similar model.

Learning and staying sharp

Reading and courses to keep building your ear and your technique. The first two are free, browser-based, and made for beginners: start there if any of the foundations feel shaky.

  • Ableton: Learning Music: an interactive, play-in-the-browser introduction to beats, melody, chords, and song structure.
  • Ableton: Learning Synths: the same hands-on approach for synthesis, from the ground up.
  • Sound on Sound: Techniques: deep, trustworthy articles on recording, mixing, and mastering.
  • iZotope: Learn: clear guides on mixing and mastering, from a maker of mastering tools.

Sounds and tools

Where to find sounds you are free to use, and instruments to make your own. Free-to-use here means the license lets you release music made with the sound. Always glance at the terms before you build a release on something.

  • Splice: a large subscription library of royalty-free samples and loops you can release with.
  • Freesound: a free, community-uploaded bank of recorded sounds. Check each sound's license.
  • Plugin Boutique: free plugins: a running collection of free instruments and effects for any DAW.

Community and organizations

Organizations connect you to the wider field of people who record, mix, and produce: their conferences, resources, and recognition. A student membership is one of the simplest ways to stop working in isolation.

  • Audio Engineering Society (AES): the professional body for audio engineering, with papers, conventions, and student membership.
  • Recording Academy: Producers & Engineers Wing: the producing and engineering arm of the organization behind the Grammys.
