The DAW

Adobe Audition

Two working styles run through audio software: editing that changes the file, like Audacity, and editing that changes a clip and leaves the file alone, like Ableton. Adobe Audition does both. It is built for shaping recorded sound rather than creating music, which is why podcasters and video editors use it and why it carries no instruments. Run the editing moves you already know in Audition and see how much of it carries over.

  6. In the Multitrack Editor, build a small mix: levels, pan, and one effect you recognize.

Two editors, two working styles you already have

Audition opens into one of two editors. The Waveform Editor works on a single audio file and edits it destructively: a cut changes the file, exactly like Audacity. The Multitrack Editor stacks clips on a timeline and edits them nondestructively: the edits are instructions layered on the clip, and the source files stay untouched, exactly like Ableton.

Three guides cover the same ground at different depths. Reach for whichever fits how you like to learn. Adobe's is the source of truth, the walkthrough is a current step-by-step, and the New School guide is a printable reference you can keep open beside you.

Start a session and bring in your sounds

Make a multitrack session with File → New → Multitrack Session. Set the Sample Rate to 48000 and set Bit Depth to 32-bit. Name the session and choose a folder inside your working folder at ~/Documents/lastname/, so the session file and its audio stay together. Audition saves the session as a .sesx file, which points at your audio.

Bring a handful of sounds from your library into the session with File → Import → File, or drag them onto the Files panel. The Files panel holds the media for this session, the same job the browser does in Ableton.

Edit a single file in the Waveform Editor

Double-click a sound in the Files panel. It opens in the Waveform Editor, the destructive side, so edits here change the file itself. You are working from the copies in your working folder, not your library originals, so it is safe to cut into them. This is the Audacity way of editing, one file, changes that stick.

Pick the Time Selection tool (press T), then click and drag across a part you want gone, a long gap or a stray noise. Press Delete. In the Waveform Editor the file closes up around the cut, the same as deleting a selection in Audacity.

Smooth the start and end so they do not click. In the top-left corner of the waveform, drag the fade handle inward to make a fade-in, and drag the handle in the top-right corner for a fade-out. This is the same fade habit you built in Audacity, where a hard edit boundary pops unless you ramp it.

Clean up a noisy recording

You show the software a slice of pure background noise, then ask it to subtract that noise from the whole file.

Open a noisy recording with File → Open. It lands in the Waveform Editor. Find a short stretch of just the background noise, with no sound you want to keep, and select one to three seconds of it. Open Effects → Noise Reduction/Restoration → Noise Reduction (Process) and click Capture Noise Print. Audition now holds a fingerprint of the noise. Select the whole file with Cmd-A, set the Noise Reduction amount to around 70 to 80 percent, press Preview to hear it, then Apply.

Keep a light touch. Pushed past about 90 percent, the reduction leaves the sound hollow and underwater. A little background left in sounds more natural than a heavily scrubbed file. Capturing a profile first, then reducing, is exactly the two-step shape from your sample-prep pipeline.

One thing that is new to you

Open a file in the Waveform Editor and switch to the Spectral Frequency Display using the view button at the top of the editor. Instead of the loudness shape you know from a waveform, you see frequency from low to high up the side and time across the bottom, with louder sounds drawn brighter. A cough, a chair squeak, or a phone buzz shows up as its own bright shape.

Select that shape with the Marquee or Lasso tool and press Delete, or use Heal Selection to fill it with the sound around it. Neither Audacity nor Ableton gave you this.

Build a small multitrack mix

Switch to the Multitrack Editor (Cmd-9). Drag a few of your sounds from the Files panel onto separate tracks. Each track has a channel strip down its left side, with a volume fader, a Mute button (M), a Solo button (S), and a pan control. Set rough levels with the faders and place sounds across the stereo field with pan. This is the Ableton mixer and the Toft console in another shape: the track fader is the channel fader, pan is pan, and solo lets you listen to one thing at a time.

Add one effect you already know. Select a track, open the Effects Rack, and add a Parametric Equalizer, the same EQ you used in Ableton under a different name. The rack also holds the compression and limiting you have met before, ready when you want them. Because this is the Multitrack side, the effect is nondestructive, so you can change or remove it at any time.

Time and pitch, the tape link again

While you are here, try stretching a clip: drag the small white triangles in its top corners. Right-click the clip, open Stretch, and look at the Stretch Properties. Varispeed changes pitch and duration together, faster means higher and shorter, like speeding up tape. That is the coupling you saw in Audacity's Change Speed. The other modes hold pitch while they stretch, the way Ableton's warp does.

Export the mix

The session file is not your finished audio. To get a sound file you can hand off, use File → Export → Multitrack Mixdown → Entire Session, choose WAV at 48 kHz, and save it into your working folder. This is the same project-versus-export split throughout: the .sesx holds your editing decisions, the exported WAV is the thing other people can play.
