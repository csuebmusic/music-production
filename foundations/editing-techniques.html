Foundations

Editing Techniques

Where each editing move lives in Audacity.

Nine core editing moves (cut, trim, splice, fade, crossfade, loop, reverse, time-stretch, pitch-shift) plus a tenth, Change Speed: the older tape-physics version of time and pitch manipulation, worth experiencing before the modern decoupled versions. This handout shows where each one lives in Audacity and gives you a hands-on exercise for each.

Follow along

To try these as you read, open Audacity and make a scratch project: File → New (Cmd + N), then File → Save Project As… somewhere convenient. A good project rate is 48 kHz. Drag any sound in to experiment on, and work freely: undo, delete tracks, try things twice. Keeping one sound in the project at a time means Cmd + A (select all) only affects what you are working on.

Before the techniques: zoom in, find a zero crossing

One thing to learn before any of the editing moves below: zoom into the waveform. Most edits look approximate when you're zoomed all the way out; zoomed in, you can see the actual shape of the wave and place a cut or fade exactly where you mean to.

The other thing to learn together with zooming is the idea of a zero crossing. The y-axis on a waveform is amplitude. The horizontal line through the middle of the track is amplitude zero, where the wave is momentarily silent on its way from positive to negative or back. A zero crossing is any point where the waveform crosses that center line.

Zero crossings matter because of click pops. If you cut a clip in the middle of a wave (where the amplitude is, say, +0.6), the playback hits +0.6 and then jumps instantly to wherever the next clip starts. That instant jump is heard as a click. If you cut at a zero crossing, the amplitude is zero on both sides of the cut, so there's no jump and no click. The habit: zoom in, find a zero crossing, cut there.

Snap a selection to zero crossings

Audacity has a built-in command that moves your selection's edges to the nearest zero crossings automatically. After you make a selection, press Z. The selection edges shift slightly (often by just a few samples) to land at zero. This is the easiest way to get clean cut boundaries without eyeballing them.

Menu path: Select → At Zero Crossings · Z

Try it

  1. In your scratch project, import any sound to practice on.
  2. Press Cmd + F to fit the whole sound in the window.
  3. Drag-select a region somewhere in the middle of the waveform. Don't worry about where the boundaries land.
  4. Press Cmd + E to zoom into your selection. Now you can see individual cycles of the wave.
  5. Look at where your selection's left and right edges fall. Are they on the center line, or partway up or down a cycle?
  6. Press Z. Watch the edges move slightly. They're now at zero crossings.
  7. Press Cmd + F to zoom back out. Delete the track before moving on (it doesn't need to stay around).
Habit to build

Every time you make a selection that you're about to cut, delete, or copy, press Z first. It takes one keystroke and prevents most click pops. Fades, which we'll cover in technique 4, are the other way to clean up edit boundaries; using both together is even better.

The ten techniques

Each technique below has the same shape: a one-line recap of what it does, the exact place to find it in Audacity, and a hands-on exercise. Work through them in order. Don't skip; the order builds.

For each exercise, import the sound (or sounds) into your scratch project (File → Import → Audio… or Cmd + Shift + I) so each appears as a new track. After you've applied the technique and listened, delete the track(s) before starting the next exercise (click the track name in the track header, then Tracks → Remove Tracks). Keeping the scratch project clean between exercises means commands like Cmd + A (select all) will only affect the sound you're currently working on. Don't try to make any one result a finished thing; just practice the move.

1 · Cut

Remove a selected region. The audio on either side closes the gap. Like deleting a phrase from the middle of a sentence.

Where in Audacity
Edit → Cut · Cmd + X
Exercise

Find a sound with someone speaking a phrase or sentence (a few words long, at most). Import it. Listen once. Then select a single word in the middle of the phrase (use your ears and the waveform to find the boundaries, drag-select with the I-beam). Press Cmd + X. Listen again.

Listen for: the cut should sound like the word was never there. The phrase should still feel coherent, just shorter. If you hear a click pop at the cut boundary, that's the edit-boundary problem; we'll fix it with fades in technique 4.

2 · Trim

Keep a selected region and remove everything else. The opposite of cut. Useful for isolating a single sound from a longer recording.

Where in Audacity
Edit → Remove Special → Trim Audio · Cmd + T
Trim is hidden

Trim is buried under Edit → Remove Special, not directly under Edit. Audacity hides it there because it's a less-frequent move. Now you know.

Exercise

Find a longer field recording (something with multiple events: birds plus traffic, water plus wind, etc.). Import it. Listen and find one specific event you like (a single bird call, one car passing, one gust of wind). Drag-select just that event. Press Cmd + T.

Listen for: everything outside your selection is gone. You've isolated one event from a busier recording. This is how musique concrète composers extract usable fragments from raw field recordings.

3 · Splice and arrange

Place sounds next to each other in time. The fundamental musique concrète move: take a fragment of one recording, place a fragment of another after it, and a third after that. The arrangement is the composition.

Where in Audacity
There's no single "splice" command. The basic recipe is to use Edit → Cut on a region from one place, then Edit → Paste at the cursor position somewhere else (on the same track or a different one).

If you want the audio you cut from to not close the gap (so the original timing is preserved), use Edit → Remove Special → Cut and leave gap (Cmd + Option + X) instead of plain Cut.

Exercise

Pick three different short sounds with different characters (for example a sharp attack, a found object, and a spoken word). Import each as its own track; these are your source tracks. Then add an empty arrangement track below them (Tracks → Add New → Stereo Track). This is where you'll assemble the splice. Copy a piece of source 1, paste it into the arrangement track at the start; copy a piece of source 2, paste it into the arrangement track after; copy a piece of source 3, paste it after that. Aim for a short phrase, maybe 5 to 10 seconds total.

Listen for: a sequence of unrelated sounds, played in the order you chose, becomes a phrase with shape. The arrangement gives it meaning. Schaeffer's Études de bruits were built this way, one splice at a time.

4 · Fade in and fade out

A volume ramp at the boundary of an edit. Fade-in starts from silence and ramps up; fade-out is the reverse. Even very short fades (10 to 50 milliseconds) prevent click pops at edit boundaries.

Where in Audacity
Effect → Fading → Fade In
Effect → Fading → Fade Out

The Effect menu groups effects by category. Fading is one of the categories; click it to expand. The fade is applied to whatever is currently selected, and its length equals the length of your selection: a 1-second selection makes a 1-second fade, a 50-millisecond selection makes a 50-millisecond fade.

Exercise

Find a long sustained sound (a drone, a held note, a long bowed sound). Import it. Select the first ~1 second. Apply Effect → Fading → Fade In. Then select the last ~1 second. Apply Effect → Fading → Fade Out. Listen.

Listen for: the sound now eases in instead of starting abruptly, and eases out instead of ending hard. Look at the waveform. The first and last seconds taper visibly. This is the move that turns a "broken edit" into a "deliberate edit."

5 · Crossfade

The overlap of a fade-out and a fade-in: one sound fades out as another fades in. The seam between two sounds becomes smooth instead of abrupt. Crossfades are how editors make hard cuts disappear.

Where in Audacity
Effect → Fading → Crossfade Clips (two clips on the same track, overlapping)

Audacity also has Effect → Fading → Crossfade Tracks for two stacked tracks (one above the other, overlapping in time). For today, we'll use Crossfade Clips, which works on two adjacent clips inside a single track.

Exercise

Pick two sounds with similar character (for example two continuous textures: maybe rain and wind, or two different street ambiences). Import both onto the same track. To put them on the same track, drag the second one onto the first track's lane. Position them so they slightly overlap, maybe by half a second. Drag-select across both clips so the overlap is in your selection. Apply Effect → Fading → Crossfade Clips.

Listen for: instead of a hard cut from sound 1 to sound 2, you hear them blend through the overlap. The seam disappears. This is one of the most useful moves in your editing toolkit; you'll use it constantly.

6 · Loop

A region played repeatedly. Useful as a working aid (loop a section while you adjust an effect) or as a compositional move (a tape-loop pattern that becomes the rhythmic backbone of a piece).

Where in Audacity
Two paths, depending on what you want:
  • Looping playback (the loop is a listening tool, the audio doesn't change): make a selection, then Transport → Looping → Set Loop to Selection (Shift + L), then Transport → Looping → Loop on/off (L) to toggle looping. Press spacebar to play; the selection plays in a loop until you stop.
  • Looping in the audio (the loop becomes part of the piece): copy a clip (Cmd + C), then paste it (Cmd + V) several times in succession. Each paste places another copy at the cursor. The loop is now baked into the track.
Exercise

Find a short rhythmic-sounding sound (a tap, a click, a brief mechanical pulse, something under a second). Import it. Try the playback-loop first: select it, set it as the loop, turn looping on, play. Listen for a few cycles, then stop. Now try the in-audio loop: copy the clip, paste it four or five times in succession on the same track. Play the result.

Listen for: a single short sound becomes a pulse. Repetition turns a static fragment into something with rhythm and momentum. This is the building block of every loop-based piece in modern music. Looping is the central organizing idea in DAWs built around it, like Ableton.

7 · Reverse

Play a sound backward. The recording is the same, but its envelope is flipped: the attack becomes the release, and the release becomes the attack. Reverse changes the direction of the sound through time, not its duration or its pitch.

Where in Audacity
Effect → Special → Reverse

Reverse lives under the Special category in the Effect menu, alongside other one-off transforms.

Exercise

Find a sound with a clear strike-then-decay shape (a struck object, a single piano note, a pluck, a snap). Import it. Listen once. Then select all of it (Cmd + A) and apply Effect → Special → Reverse. Listen.

Listen for: instead of a sharp hit followed by a fade, you hear the sound rush in and end abruptly. The envelope has flipped end-to-end. Reversed strikes are one of the most recognizable sounds in musique concrète; Schaeffer worked them by physically reversing tape reels, and the resulting "swelling rush" became part of the genre's vocabulary.

8 · Change Speed

Change the duration and pitch of a sound together, the way tape does. Slow it down and the pitch drops; speed it up and the pitch rises. This is the original time-and-pitch move from before digital audio existed.

Where in Audacity
Effect → Pitch and Tempo → Change Speed and Pitch…

This was the only kind of time-and-pitch operation available for most of the twentieth century. If a tape engineer slowed a recording down, the pitch dropped with it; that's just physics. The reading covered the modern decoupled versions (techniques 9 and 10 below), but it's worth doing the coupled version first, because it's the most intuitive starting point and because it sounds different.

Exercise

Find a long sustained or continuous sound (something with at least a few seconds of stable sustain). Import it. Select all of it. Apply Effect → Pitch and Tempo → Change Speed and Pitch… In the dialog, set Percent Change to -50 (which both doubles the duration and drops the pitch by roughly an octave). Click Apply. Listen. Then undo (Cmd + Z) and try +100 (which both halves the duration and raises the pitch by roughly an octave). Listen again.

Listen for: the duration and pitch both move together, in the same direction. Slower means lower; faster means higher. The two are coupled, exactly the way they would be if you were physically slowing or speeding a tape reel. Hold onto how this sounds. The next two techniques will decouple them, and you'll hear what that decoupling does.

9 · Time-stretch

Change the duration of a sound without changing its pitch. A modern signal-processing operation that decouples what tape kept coupled.

Where in Audacity
Effect → Pitch and Tempo → Change Tempo…

Audacity calls this "Change Tempo." A dialog opens; you can change the tempo by a percentage or by entering a new length in seconds. The pitch stays exactly where it was. This is what the previous technique would do if you could somehow disconnect duration from pitch, which is exactly what modern signal processing makes possible.

Exercise

Use the same sound you just used for Change Speed. Either undo (Cmd + Z) until the sound is back to its original, or delete the modified track and re-import the sound fresh. Then select all of it and apply Effect → Pitch and Tempo → Change Tempo… Set Percent Change to -50. Click Apply. Listen.

Listen for: the sound is now twice as long, just like Change Speed at -50%. But the pitch is exactly where it was before, not an octave lower. This is the decoupling. The same time change, with no pitch consequence. That's the thing tape couldn't do.

10 · Pitch-shift

Change the pitch of a sound without changing its duration. The complement to time-stretch.

Where in Audacity
Effect → Pitch and Tempo → Change Pitch…

The dialog lets you specify the change in semitones, percent, or as a from-to musical pitch. Semitones is the most musically intuitive: +12 semitones is one octave up, -12 is one octave down.

Exercise

Use the same kind of long-sustain sound (a fresh import, or undo back to the original). Select all of it and apply Effect → Pitch and Tempo → Change Pitch… Set Semitones to -12 (one octave down). Click Apply. Listen. Then undo (Cmd + Z) and try +7 (a perfect fifth up). Listen again.

Listen for: the duration is unchanged, but the pitch has moved. Three operations, three different relationships between time and pitch: Change Speed couples them (both move together), Change Tempo moves time only, Change Pitch moves pitch only. Together these three give you full control over either dimension, the other dimension, or both.
